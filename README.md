# HCI Evaluation of AI-Powered Smart Fitting Rooms at Reformation, Covent Garden

**Module:** Human Computer Interaction — PGDip Artificial Intelligence (Human Centred)  
**Institution:** Bournemouth University, UK  
**Grade:** Near First Class  
**Year:** 2024  

---

## Overview

This study conducts a rigorous mixed-methods Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) evaluation of Reformation's AI-powered in-store smart fitting room and shop floor application at their Covent Garden flagship store — a real, commercially deployed AI system used by customers daily.

Reformation markets its in-store technology as a smart, AI-driven personalised shopping experience. This research critically evaluates whether it lives up to that claim — particularly for users with disabilities — and proposes evidence-based product improvements.

---

## Research Questions

1. How usable is Reformation's AI-powered fitting room system for the general public?
2. Does the system meet legal accessibility standards under W3C/WCAG 2.2 and the European Accessibility Act?
3. What improvements would make the system more inclusive, accessible, and commercially effective?
4. What can other retailers learn from Reformation's approach to in-store AI?

---

## Methodology

Five distinct HCI research methods were applied to the same deployed AI system, enabling triangulation of findings:

| Method | Purpose |
|--------|---------|
| **Soft Systems Methodology (SSM/CATWOE)** | Systemic analysis of the socio-technical environment, stakeholders, and transformation goals |
| **W3C/WCAG 2.2 Accessibility Testing** | Colour/contrast, image alternative text, and zoom compliance testing for users with disabilities |
| **System Usability Scale (SUS)** | Quantitative usability scoring across 5 distinct tasks with real participants |
| **Nielsen's Heuristic Evaluation** | Expert evaluation against 10 usability principles, adapted for retail AI contexts |
| **In-depth Qualitative Interviews** | Semi-structured interviews with retail business stakeholders on appetite for AI-powered retail |

---

## Key Findings

### Accessibility
- Reformation's fitting room system **failed** the W3C colour/contrast test — no grayscale mode available
- **Failed** the image alternative text test — Siri/voice commands were disabled on in-store devices
- Button sizes were compliant with W3C 24×24px guidelines but difficult to interact with physically
- Users with visual impairments would be unable to independently use the system
- Accessibility failures of this kind cost the UK retail industry an estimated **£120 billion in lost sales**

### Usability (SUS)
- Tasks 2 and 4 scored high — browsing and selecting items is intuitive
- Task 5 (finding product information) scored lowest — a significant UX gap
- Mixed results on calling for staff assistance, suggesting inconsistent user mental models

### Heuristic Evaluation
- Strong: visibility of system status, real-world match, user freedom and control
- Weak: error prevention relies entirely on staff demonstration rather than in-system guidance
- No help or documentation available within the application itself (violates Heuristic 10)

### Interview Insights
- Stakeholders highlighted sizing inconsistency as a problem AI cannot yet fully solve
- Strong appetite for pre-store booking and mobile notifications among participants
- Human connection remains valued for high-stakes purchases (weddings, formal wear)

---

## Product Recommendations

1. **Pre-store booking integration** — allow customers to select items before arrival; fitting room ready on entry
2. **AI size recommendation engine** — body measurement data to suggest sizing, reducing returns
3. **Real-time mobile queue notifications** — customers notified on their own device when fitting room is ready
4. **Accessibility overhaul** — enable VoiceOver/Siri, add grayscale mode, improve alt text across all devices
5. **Search and filter functionality** — customers currently must scroll entire catalogue; search is essential

---

## Significance

This research demonstrates how human-centred evaluation of AI retail systems can simultaneously:
- Identify legal compliance failures (WCAG, European Accessibility Act)
- Surface commercial opportunities (£120bn accessibility gap)
- Generate actionable 0→1 product improvements
- Contribute to more inclusive AI design for underserved user groups
